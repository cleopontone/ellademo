The problem

Hotel underwriting has a structural blind spot.

When an investor underwrites a hotel acquisition, the industry-standard input is STR benchmarking data — RevPAR, ADR, and occupancy for the existing competitive set. It tells you how the market is performing today.

STR has one structural limitation it cannot overcome: it only reflects supply that already exists and is already operating. It cannot show you:

  • A 72-key hotel approved next door that won't open for two years
  • Budget motels converting to housing, quietly tightening supply
  • A national luxury operator entering the submarket through a quiet acquisition
  • Regulatory shifts reshaping the competitive map — coastal-resilience scrutiny, motel-to-housing pressure

By the time a buyer closes and ramps to stabilization — typically years 2 to 3 — the market they actually compete in looks nothing like the STR snapshot they underwrote against. New supply lands right as they're trying to push ADR and occupancy, capping the upside the model assumed.

The result: deals get priced against the market as it is, not the market as it will be. The single biggest swing factor in hotel returns — future supply — is the one thing the standard toolkit can't surface.
